Understanding 53-TET Chord Notation

The Microtonal Explorer Project uses an extended chord naming convention that captures the nuanced interval qualities available in 53-tone equal temperament. This naming system builds upon traditional chord notation while introducing additional symbols to represent the microtonal variations of thirds, fifths, and sevenths.

In this system, chords are classified based on the quality of their third, fifth, and seventh intervals, with specific symbols indicating the precise tuning of each interval. This notation allows for clear communication of chord structures that extend beyond the 12-TET system.

Interval Quality Prefixes

The following prefixes are used to indicate the quality of intervals:

  • S^ - supermajor (larger than major)
  • ^ - upmajor (slightly larger than major)
  • M - major (standard major interval)
  • vM - downmajor (slightly smaller than major)
  • NM - neutral-major (between major and neutral)
  • N - neutral (exactly between major and minor)
  • Nm - neutral-minor (between neutral and minor)
  • m - minor (standard minor interval)
  • vm - downminor (slightly smaller than minor)
  • sm - subminor (smaller than minor)

Additionally, special fifth qualities are indicated as follows:

  • + - augmented fifth
  • ø - diminished fifth

Interval Distribution in 53-TET

The 53-TET system divides the octave into 53 equal parts, providing a rich palette of intervals that closely approximate just intonation ratios while maintaining the flexibility of equal temperament.

Interval Quality Relationships

The 53-TET system enables precise expression of interval qualities that fall between the traditional major and minor distinctions. The naming system follows a logical hierarchy of interval qualities, from largest to smallest:

Interval Hierarchy (Third, Fifth, Seventh)

  1. Supermajor (S^) - The largest interval quality, more expanded than a standard major interval
  2. Upmajor (^) - Slightly larger than a standard major interval
  3. Major (M) - Traditional major interval as found in standard Western harmony
  4. Downmajor (vM) - Slightly smaller than a standard major interval
  5. Neutral-major (NM) - Between major and neutral, leaning toward major
  6. Neutral (N) - Precisely between major and minor (quarter tone)
  7. Neutral-minor (Nm) - Between neutral and minor, leaning toward minor
  8. Minor (m) - Traditional minor interval as found in standard Western harmony
  9. Downminor (vm) - Slightly smaller than a standard minor interval
  10. Subminor (sm) - The smallest interval quality, more compressed than a standard minor interval

This hierarchy allows for subtle expressive shadings that are unavailable in 12-TET systems.

Application in Composition

These extended interval qualities offer composers unprecedented control over harmonic color and expression. When applied to modal interchange, they create a rich palette of chord relationships that extends far beyond the traditional major/minor system.

The following are some practical applications of this extended chord system:

  • Microtonal Voice Leading - Smoother voice leading through adjacent harmonic spaces
  • Extended Modal Expressivity - More precise expression of modal characteristics from global musical traditions
  • Harmonic Microtuning - Fine tuning of harmonic tension and resolution
  • Cross-Cultural Harmonic Integration - Bridging Western harmonic practices with non-Western tuning systems
